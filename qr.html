The sky is a Clifford attractor — a strange attractor from chaos theory, named for Clifford Pickover, who popularized these iterated-map forms in his 1990s books on chaos and computational beauty. Two deceptively simple equations x′ = sin(a·y) + c·cos(a·x)   y′ = sin(b·x) + d·cos(b·y) are iterated here by 90,000 particles. The motion is fully deterministic — no randomness in the map — yet it never repeats, and the points condense onto these fractal filaments. The four constants slowly morph between hand-picked sets, so the form keeps dissolving and reforming: a trick carried forward from my own ChaosRenders, which shifted the same constants in C# back in 2020 — its home set (−1.7, 1.8, −0.9, −0.4) leads the cycle. Chaos isn't disorder; it's structure you couldn't have predicted.
